Shrek Dialogue in 3 Charts

A breakdown of which each character contributes to the trilogy.

By Shane Caldwell, Mac Gray, and David Duffrin

Creating numbers you can crunch from a movie isn't obvious, and gaining information about the narrative from those numbers is more challenging still. However, perhaps there are things we can learn from a data-driven exploration of our film that we can't learn anywhere else.

The Pudding (who we love) did a piece called The Largest Ever Analysis of Film Dialogue by Gender: 2,000 scripts, 25,000 actors, 4 million lines, which ended up looking at over 2,000 different films looking at the gender of characters. They used the same dataset to come up with She Giggles, He Gallops, an analysis of stage direction. It was (and is) really dope.

While both those analyses are impressive in their scope, it loses some of the personality in the film. That makes sense, of course, their interest is largely in how much of the script is dedicated to male vs female roles. The actual stories there aren't relevant to the question they're trying to answer.

Is that all that can be done with the textual data from scripts? Or is it possible to learn something about the movies narrative structure and character relationships through data analysis and visualization?

To answer this question, we knew one movie wouldn't be enough. More lines means more data, after all. But to focus on character analysis we need the same characters to show up from multiple movies. So we needed a franchise, then. With that decided, there was no question about which to choose.

Shrek is something of a pop culture icon. Originally based off of a 30 page children's book written by William Steig, it became a box office smash hit that's spawned multiple sequels, a really weird internet precense, and it's own annual festival. As Shrek celebrates his 17th birthday, we here at Trashmission wanted to peak under the hood and see what exactly gave the Dreamworks reviving franchise such staying power, and see if our visualization techniques could result in any interesting statements about the narrative or character relationship's from the Shrek films. Maybe even films in general?

Methodology

We tracked down the scripts to the first three Shrek movies (hereby referred to as "the trilogy"*). We considered just using the per-character lines from each movie, but we wanted to experiment with time a little bit. To do that, we ended up downloading subtitle files for each film.

Subtitle files are structured with the dialogue to be put on screen, and when to put it on screen, but don't have the character's name. Thus began the painstaking process of adding the character name to each line spoken by each character, and then transforming the .srt into a filetype we could do further data processing on. Beauty requires sacrifice.

For some of the analysis on per-character dialogue, the choice was made to filter out characters who didn't have a certain number of lines.

How smart is everyone?

Reading level of each character in for each character who had over 20 lines.

Flesch–Kincaid Grade Level is a score assigned to speech or text to decide how difficult it is to read and give it a grade level score based on this difficulty. The primary measurements used are word length and sentence length.

Main characters end up looking particularly bad here - why is that? For one, main character's tend to engage in a lot more back and forth dialogue. This can be represented by many short, frequently cut off sentences.

For example, consider the following dialogue with Shrek and Donkey from Shrek, along with their associated reading level score:

Whoa, that was really scary. And if you don’t mind me saying, if that don’t work your breath’ll certainly get the job done. Listen, little Donkey, take a look at me. What am I? Ahhh…really green?! No! I’m an ogre! You know – grab your torch and pitchforks! Doesn’t that bother you? Nope. Really? Really really. Oh...? Man, I like you. What’s your name? Shrek.

It's not looking good for our heroes, here. With these kinds of scenes, partiuclarly common in comedies, it's no wonder our main characters, with the greatest number of lines, come out worse for ware.

The character's with the highest reading level are Merlin, the wizard from Shrek 3 and the Magic Mirror from Shrek 1. Both have a role as "witty" characters without necessarily being slapstick comic relief like Donkey, so this makes sense. See this line from the mirror:

Bachelorette number two is a cape-wearing girl from the Land of Fantasy. Although she lives with seven other men, she's not easy. Just kiss her frozen, dead lips and find out what a live wire she is. Give it up for Snow White!

and this line from Merlin:

Greetings, cosmic children of the universe. Welcome to my serenity circle. Please leave all bad vibes outside the healing vortex.

Neither of them are Shakespearean, but their sentence structure is a little longer.
