Shrek Dialogue in 3 Charts

A retrospective on what your favorite characters contributed to the trilogy.

By Shane Caldwell, Mac Gray, and David Duffrin

How smart is everyone?

Reading level of each character in for each character who had over 20 lines.

Flesch–Kincaid Grade Level is a score assigned to speech or text to decide how difficult it is to read and give it a grade level score based on this difficulty. The primary measurements used are word length and sentence length.

Main characters end up looking particularly bad here - why is that? For one, main character's tend to engage in a lot more back and forth dialogue. This can be represented by many short, frequently cut off sentences.

For example, consider the following dialogue with Shrek and Donkey from Shrek, along with their associated reading level score:

Whoa, that was really scary. And if you don’t mind me saying, if that don’t work your breath’ll certainly get the job done. Listen, little Donkey, take a look at me. What am I? Ahhh…really green?! No! I’m an ogre! You know – grab your torch and pitchforks! Doesn’t that bother you? Nope. Really? Really really. Oh...? Man, I like you. What’s your name? Shrek.

It's not looking good for our heroes, here. With these kinds of scenes, partiuclarly common in comedies, it's no wonder our main characters, with the greatest number of lines, come out worse for ware.

The character's with the highest reading level are Merlin, the wizard from Shrek 3 and the Magic Mirror from Shrek 1. Both have a role as "witty" characters without necessarily being slapstick comic relief like Donkey, so this makes sense. See this line from the mirror:

Bachelorette number two is a cape-wearing girl from the Land of Fantasy. Although she lives with seven other men, she's not easy. Just kiss her frozen, dead lips and find out what a live wire she is. Give it up for Snow White!

and this line from Merlin:

Greetings, cosmic children of the universe. Welcome to my serenity circle. Please leave all bad vibes outside the healing vortex.

Neither of them are Shakespearean, but their sentence structure is a little longer.
